## Pricing Principles

### 1. Full-Service Premium Justifies Higher Prices
Nova Kingdom includes delivery + setup + teardown. Budget competitors who charge "pickup only" or charge extra for delivery cannot directly compare. Always communicate this in pricing conversations.

When a customer says "that's expensive":
- "That price includes delivery to your door, full setup, and we come back and take everything down. Most companies charge extra for that or make you pick it up."

### 2. Demand-Based Pricing Signals

| Signal | Action |
|--------|--------|
| Unit fully booked 3+ weekends in a row | Test 10-15% price increase next season |
| Unit rarely booked despite marketing | Check if price is too high OR if it's a visibility/marketing issue |
| Peak dates (July 4th, long weekends) | Consider peak pricing (+10-20%) |
| Off-season (Oct-Apr) | Consider slight discount to incentivize bookings |

### 3. Package Value Engineering
Packages should feel like a deal vs. à la carte — but still be more profitable per hour because they lock in higher commitment.

Quick check: Does each package save the customer at least 10% vs. booking units separately? If not, the package isn't compelling.

## Critical Rules
- **Never discount publicly.** If you need to incentivize, offer added value (free lawn game, extended hours) rather than reducing the quoted price.
- **Raise prices gradually** — 5-10% per season for in-demand units is natural and expected.
- **Price floors exist for a reason.** Below $210 for any full-service delivery rental makes the business unprofitable at scale.
- **Test price increases on new units first** — Crown Rush 42 has no price anchoring, so $450 isn't carved in stone. If demand is strong, it can go higher.
- **Always know what your costs are.** Pricing without knowing your cost per booking is guessing.
